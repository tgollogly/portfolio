How it's built — and why

The problem

Extreme heat is the deadliest weather hazard, but a raw temperature alone can't tell you whether a place is in a genuine heat dome — a persistent high-pressure anomaly well above the local norm — or just a normally hot climate. This tool answers both: how dangerous is the heat right now, and how far above this location's own historical baseline is it?

Detecting the dome

The anomaly check pulls a 30-year daily climatology for the exact location and calendar week from the Open-Meteo archive (ERA5 reanalysis). It builds a distribution of historical max temperatures for that time of year, then scores today's forecast max against it — reporting the degrees above normal and the historical percentile. A heat dome is flagged when today sits at or above roughly the 90th percentile and a set margin above the long-term mean, so a hot desert in summer isn't falsely flagged while a true anomaly is.

Stack & why I chose it

I used Open-Meteo for both live and archive data because it's free, key-free, CORS-friendly and returns apparent ('feels-like') temperature directly — so the whole thing deploys as a static file. The map is MapLibre GL JS (open-source, no token, unlike Mapbox) with free AWS elevation tiles for real 3D terrain, and RainViewer for live radar. The 24-hour chart is hand-drawn SVG rather than a charting library, to avoid a dependency and stay fast.

Trade-offs & what's next

The climatology call adds one extra request and a short wait, so the live readout renders first and the dome verdict fills in when the history returns. It's fully client-side with no persistence, and radar shows the latest frame rather than a loop. Next: frame-by-frame radar playback and official severe-weather alert feeds.
