Case study

How it's built — and why

The problem

Extreme heat is the deadliest weather hazard, yet judging the real risk for a place means stitching together temperature, humidity and radar from different sources. I wanted one screen that answers ‘how dangerous is the heat here right now?’ for anywhere on earth.

Stack & why I chose it

I used the Open-Meteo API because it's free, needs no API key, is CORS-friendly and returns apparent (‘feels-like’) temperature directly — so the whole thing deploys as a static file with no cost or sign-up. The map is MapLibre GL JS (open-source, no access token, unlike Mapbox) with free AWS elevation tiles for real 3D terrain, and RainViewer for live radar — both also key-free. The 24-hour chart is hand-drawn SVG rather than a charting library, to avoid a dependency and keep it fast and self-contained.

Key decisions

Warnings are driven by apparent temperature, not raw temperature, because ‘feels-like’ reflects actual human heat risk; those values map onto NWS-style categories from Comfortable to Extreme Danger. ‘Auto-watch’ re-fetches every five minutes with setInterval. Every external call is wrapped in try/catch, so if the map or radar fails the readout and warning still work.

Trade-offs & what's next

It's fully client-side with no persistence, and radar shows the latest frame rather than a full animation loop, to stay simple and reliable. Next: frame-by-frame radar playback and official severe-weather alert feeds.

Data — Weather: open-meteo.com · Radar: RainViewer · Terrain: AWS elevation tiles · Map: MapLibre GL. Heat-risk bands are indicative (apparent-temperature, NWS-style categories) — not an official public-health warning.
